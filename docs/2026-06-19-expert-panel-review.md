# OpenClaw RFC 0010: A Decision Memo on Pipeline Substrate, Governed Mode, and the Test-Harnesses Plan

**Author:** David Steeves
**Date:** 2026-06-18
**Doc-status:** Draft for review (post-panel synthesis)

## TL;DR

RFC 0010's single durable contribution is the control-plane / agent-plane boundary; the pipeline-substrate framing is structurally reasonable but mispriced, mislabeled, and currently shipped as universal when the bench shows it must be opt-in. The follow-up test-harnesses plan is well-shaped but overscoped for an RFC that no maintainer has acknowledged, and it smuggles in two designs (clarifying-prompts seam, multi-team policy composition) that do not yet appear in the parent RFC. The correct next move is to split RFC 0010, fold the governed-mode reframing into the canonical doc as a v3, ship a 100-line proof rather than a 6.5-hour harness, and stop posting until a maintainer engages — meanwhile fixing the four blocking architectural defects the panel surfaced (analyst co-residency, TOCTOU, escalate-to-pass, operator-asserted exemption).

## Customer

The real customer of RFC 0010 is not the hobbyist parent running OpenClaw on a laptop, and it is not the regulated multi-tenant operator. The real customer of RFC 0010 today is **a single OpenClaw maintainer with a triage queue**. That maintainer needs to be able to read this RFC in fifteen minutes and form a defensible opinion on whether the change is worth landing. Every architectural choice, every section of prose, every PR comment, every harness artifact is either serving that customer or competing with them for attention. The downstream customers (parent on a 2019 MacBook Air, hosted multi-tenant operator, HIPAA-adjacent deployment) are the *eventual* customers of whatever RFC 0010 turns into — but they cannot be served at all until the maintainer says "yes, this direction is worth pursuing." Right now the RFC, the bench, the PR reply, and the harness plan together demand more than fifteen minutes of focused attention to evaluate, and that is the customer problem the panel surfaced from every angle.

## Context

RFC 0010 proposes that OpenClaw's session and transcript substrate be shaped as a three-stage data pipeline (raw → processed → curated), that the stage boundaries become explicit "seams" where analyst skills can read, classify, transform, hold, or block payloads, and that the substrate, the analyst registry, and the Policy artifacts be reserved to a Gateway control plane that the agent plane cannot rewrite. The evidence shape is asserted to reuse RFC 0003. The proposal is storage-neutral by construction — stages can collapse to a single SQLite table or fan out to separate stores.

The work as it stands across the four artifacts is the following. The RFC body bundles three normative shifts (pipeline shape, seam contract, control-plane boundary) into one document and defers eight named decisions to follow-up RFCs that do not exist. A bench was run on an M5 Pro with ten concurrent agents across three payload classes (512 B, 5 MB, 100 MB), measuring file-share, sqlite-flat, and a faithful 3-stage pipeline implementation. The bench REPORT and the PR reply (openclaw/rfcs#11 issuecomment-4656114434, posted 2026-06-09) report that the pipeline shape is ~4× slower than baseline at chat-message scale, ~35× at 5 MB, and ~180× at 100 MB. The PR reply does not retreat from the RFC — it reframes it. Instead of "pipeline substrate for every OpenClaw instance," the reply proposes a per-claw "governed mode" setting where Gateway substitutes a pipe-end for the claw's storage handle, the claw remains unaware, and ACL enforcement lives outside the claw's blast radius. The analog given is Istio's sidecar. Five questions are asked of maintainers, ranging from "has anyone tried this?" to "what should the empty-seam default be?"

The follow-up plan (plans/2026-06-19-policy-harness-extension.md) describes a public companion repo, `openclaw-test-harnesses`, that would ship a control-plane config schema, a clarifying-prompts decision-tree schema, system and shared-lib policy snippets, two reference review teams (`pii-reviewer` and `phi-reviewer`) with Synthea-shaped and Workday-shaped mock data, a policy-eval harness producing verdict tapes, a perf harness that adds a `pipeline-real-claws` variant to openclaw-pipeline-bench, and a comparison report generator. The plan estimates ~6.5 hours and explicitly disclaims that none of this proposes new RFCs. A second plan (plans/pr-comment-when-impl-ships.md) drafts the follow-up PR comment that would announce the harness, with a Proof Shape table deep-linking to specific test files at a pinned `rfc-0010-v1` tag. No maintainer has replied to the original PR; the 2026-06-09 reply currently sits as a one-author monologue.

Eight expert reviews — an Amazon principal in the Werner mold, a Microsoft SDE with Skype/Teams scars, a Google SDE from the Borg + Cloud DLP world, a DocuSign eSign engineer who has been deposed about audit trails, two pentesters (one general-purpose, one focused on timing), an OSS old-school reviewer in the Linus tradition, and an OSS RFC-process specialist — were assembled and asked to read the same four artifacts. Their findings converge sharply on four architectural defects, diverge on one question (whether to keep the pipeline framing at all), and converge again on the process critique. This memo synthesizes them.

## Findings

### The control-plane / agent-plane boundary is the strongest claim in the RFC and the most under-defended

Every reviewer named the segregation argument as the single most important contribution in RFC 0010. The DocuSign reviewer called it "the most important architectural claim in the document." The old-school reviewer extracted the line "a supervisor that the supervised can rewrite is not a supervisor" as the one sentence in the doc that gets quoted later. The OSS-process reviewer recommended lifting it out and giving it its own RFC. The Google reviewer endorsed it as the right Borg/admission-controller instinct. There is no dissent on this point.

There is also no dissent on the fact that the RFC does not actually deliver the property it claims. The Amazon reviewer's framing is the sharpest: the RFC names a Saltzer-Schroeder non-bypassable-mediation property and then describes it in prose without specifying any mechanism that would force it. No principal model. No signer chain on Policy artifacts. No capability handle. No process boundary. The Gateway "knows" a call originated from agent-plane through unspecified means. "Write-restricted from whom, enforced by what" — filesystem perms, a different SQLite file with the same uid, a different Python module in the same interpreter — is never named. The pentester reviewer drove the same point through the explicit Non-Goal in RFC §2 line 119-121: "Analyst skills run in the existing skill runtime. The trust improvement comes from where they run in the pipeline, not from how they are sandboxed." The RFC's most load-bearing claim is structurally contradicted by its own Non-Goal: the supervisor lives inside the supervised's blast radius by design.

**Position: The reviewers are right and this is a blocking defect.** A capability model — every skill runs under a principal, every Policy artifact has a signer, every Gateway operation requires a handle the agent plane cannot synthesize, with a process boundary at minimum — must be named in RFC 0010 itself before any maintainer ack is sought. The current text is policy-by-convention dressed as policy-by-construction, and that gap will be the first thing a real reviewer surfaces.

### TOCTOU between seam evaluation and downstream visibility is the defining correctness property and is currently deferred

The Amazon, Google, and timing-pentester reviewers all independently flagged the unresolved-question on time-of-check/time-of-use as the defining correctness property of a pipeline-as-policy-substrate. The Amazon reviewer's words: "This is the *defining* correctness property of a pipeline-as-policy-substrate and it's marked TBD." The timing pentester escalated to "blocking" and pointed out that the bench does not measure it at all — pipeline-fullcopy was benchmarked with SELECT+DELETE but never under concurrent agent-plane readers on the source stage during analyst evaluation.

The mechanism the reviewers want is concrete and converges across personas: the substrate, not the stage plugin, must guarantee that no agent-plane reader sees a row in stage N+1 until the evidence record for the N→N+1 seam is durable and references the destination-stage row id. This is a single-writer commit-evidence-then-commit-row sequence under Gateway control. The Google reviewer would express it as snapshot isolation per stage or an explicit evidence-before-read barrier; the timing pentester would express it as a bench variant that asserts no pre-evidence reads occur. Either expression delivers the same property. Deferring this to a follow-up RFC means the current bench numbers measure the cost of the happy path, not the cost of the architecture being safe.

**Position: This is also blocking, and it must move out of "unresolved questions" and into normative text before the harness work continues.** The cost of the lock is part of the cost of the substrate. Until it is benched, "structurally sound" is unverified.

### The verdict algebra is a partial order presented as a total order — and the escalate-to-pass path is a pre-staged DoS

The Google reviewer named the algebra defect with precision: `block` outranks `escalate` outranks `transform` outranks `pass` is a total-order statement, but `transform` mutates the payload while `escalate` marks it pending-human-review and continues downstream. These verdicts are not comparable on a single severity axis. Two analysts at the same seam — one emitting transform-redact-SSN, one emitting escalate-for-human-review — produce a state that the algebra does not cover. K8s mutating admission learned this; the RFC has not.

The timing pentester escalated the related concern that escalate has a timeout-to-pass terminal state: "a Policy-configured timeout converts unresolved escalations to `block` or `pass` per operator choice." This is the attacker's preferred verdict. Any operator who configures timeout→pass (and they will, because timeout→block breaks availability) hands an attacker a trivial conversion of every block-worthy event into a pass by DoSing the human-review channel. The Microsoft reviewer surfaced an adjacent failure mode the RFC also does not address: the analyst-throws case, the analyst-OOMs case, the regex-with-catastrophic-backtracking case, the network-dep-timeout case. The verdict taxonomy covers happy-path verdicts and ignores the failure modes that dominate the actual on-call queue.

There is no disagreement among reviewers here. The fixes are: kill timeout→pass and replace with timeout→block or timeout→bounded-queue-then-fail-closed; specify the lattice as two axes (severity × mutation) with the merge function named for each cell; add a §Failure Modes section to the RFC covering analyst throw, timeout, OOM, malformed verdict, and the fail-mode default per seam.

### The empty-evidence stamp default is wrong, and every reviewer says so

This was the rare item where every persona converged on the same answer. The RFC currently defaults to "empty seam → emit an observability stamp" with Policy able to declare certain seams as requiring at least one analyst. The Amazon, Google, DocuSign, pentester, and timing-pentester reviewers all flipped this without coordination: sensitive seams should fail closed by default, with Policy opt-in to downgrade to observability. The Google reviewer made the comparison explicit — K8s `ValidatingAdmissionPolicy`, Cloud DLP InspectTemplates, and AWS IAM all default the other direction; "permissive default + Policy opt-in to strict" is the misconfigured-cloud-breach pattern of the last decade. The Microsoft reviewer would have it both ways but per-seam, not globally. The DocuSign reviewer added a useful refinement: distinguish between "configured-empty," "registry-empty-by-policy," and "registry-empty-by-error" as three sub-states. The timing pentester added the cryptographic refinement: bind the empty stamp to a hash of `(seam_id, sorted_analyst_skill_ids_at_evaluation, policy_artifact_hash)` so a stage plugin cannot forge "I checked the empty registry" when it actually skipped a populated one.

**Position: Flip the default. Ship strict-by-default at v1. This is the rare quick-win that costs nothing in implementation and buys real safety posture.**

### The clarifying-prompts seam is the most dangerous surface in the package, and it is not even in the RFC

The pentester, Microsoft, DocuSign, and timing-pentester reviewers all surfaced the clarifying-prompts seam — currently living only in the harness plan — as the single most exploitable design in the artifacts. The pentester called it a confused-deputy by design. The mechanism: plan sanity gate #3 specifies that `clarifications.phi-detection = no` suppresses the PHI block analyst on rows that contain MRN-shaped data. "Logged but not enforced." An attacker who can influence the upload metadata — a compromised upload skill, a prompt-injection on the LLM that fills out the prompt, a CI tool that auto-answers — disables the PHI control on their own payloads. The Microsoft reviewer added the consent-UX critique from Teams: end-users cannot answer "does this contain PHI?" at upload time, they will habituate-yes-click within two weeks, and the regulatory exposure when they click "no" is the company's. The DocuSign reviewer added the HIPAA framing: this is exactly how covered entities get themselves in trouble, and "did the covered entity have reasonable safeguards" is a hard sell when the safeguard stands down on operator click.

The convergent reframing is: clarifying answers ADD analyst skills to a seam, never SKIP them. Policy reads metadata as a hint, not as an exemption. If the exemption path must exist, it has to be a signed attestation by an authenticated operator principal — not a checkbox.

**Position: The harness must not ship the current sanity gate.** Inverting the semantics keeps the operator-intent-capture wedge (which Microsoft and the OSS-process reviewers correctly identified as the actual novel surface) while killing the bypass. The OSS-process reviewer's separate critique is also right: this seam does not appear in RFC 0010 and is being smuggled in through the harness as a fait accompli, which is exactly the architecture-astronaut pattern.

### The bench is honest about what it measured and dishonest about what it didn't

The Amazon reviewer's framing here is the most useful: the structural claim ("the 3-stage shape costs ~4× at small payloads") is credible, but `p50 = 5.25 ms` is not a production-representative number. The M5 Pro SSD is faster than the median user's drive. tempdir on macOS is APFS-on-SSD with no fsync contention. There is no cold-cache run. There is no concurrent fsync pressure. The Microsoft reviewer drove the related concern that the REPORT.md headline ("cheap at the payload sizes today's claws actually emit") is doing too much work — the actual numbers describe a multiplier that grows nearly two orders of magnitude with payload, and reviewers reading the headline will internalize the wrong mental model. The timing pentester added the adversarial-bench critique: every variant measures a well-behaved analyst with a 1ms `asyncio.sleep`; nothing measures a slow analyst, an escalate-DoS, a malicious transform, or a post-hoc tape rewrite.

There is no reviewer disagreement here. The bench needs: a constrained-substrate run (fio-induced 100 IOPS background load, 5400rpm-equivalent disk, cold cache start), an adversarial-variants run (slow analyst, escalate-all, malicious-transform-then-classify), and a REPORT headline rewrite that leads with the multiplier curve instead of "cheap." The DocuSign reviewer additionally surfaced that pipeline-fullcopy deletes source-stage rows on promotion, which is a performance win in the bench and an evidence-shape regression for any forensic deployment.

### The governed-mode reframing is the strongest idea in the package and is buried in a PR comment

This is the rare unanimous endorsement across all eight reviewers. The Amazon reviewer called the per-claw governed-mode pivot "the strongest idea in the whole thread." The Microsoft reviewer called it "the right shape and the right analog to a sidecar pattern." The Google reviewer connected it to the admission-webhook story. The OSS-old-school reviewer called it the best thing in the whole tree. The OSS-process reviewer was the most pointed about what to do with it: roll it into RFC 0010 as a v3, not a buried comment.

The disagreement is on the *mechanism*, and it is sharp. The Amazon reviewer pointed out that Istio's sidecar works because the kernel-enforced network namespace is the boundary — the app process literally cannot talk to the network except through the sidecar — and that "Gateway substitutes a pipe-end inside the same Python process" is not a security boundary, it is a stylistic convention. The Microsoft reviewer made the same point: the analyst skills "run in the existing skill runtime" per the explicit Non-Goal, so a compromised analyst is co-resident with the agent runtime it supervises. The pentester pushed hardest: a determined claw doesn't write to its store handle, it shells out, opens its own socket, writes to `/tmp`, or exfils via DNS. Service meshes assume cooperative workloads, and so does this. For parental-monitoring on a hobbyist's machine that is fine; for hosted multi-tenant the framing is misleading.

**Position: Both reviewers are right and the reframing is right.** Governed-mode is the correct architectural pattern; the mechanism is currently a sentence rather than a design. The right move is to roll governed-mode into the RFC body AND specify the substitution mechanism honestly: in-process pipe substitution is a "policy by convention" mode appropriate for hobbyist/single-claw deployments; subprocess-with-FD-handoff is the "policy by construction" mode required for multi-tenant. Both modes can coexist as a Policy declaration.

### Multi-tenancy cannot remain a footnote

The Microsoft reviewer was unambiguous: "the moment one Gateway serves two tenants, analyst-roster bleed is a P0 privacy incident, not a P3 unresolved question." The Amazon reviewer connected this to the sakAI venture-studio context — every portfolio product is a tenant. The Google reviewer's lattice critique was framed in admission-controller language but the multi-tenancy concern is the same shape. The RFC's current text declares multi-tenancy out of scope; the harness plan ships two review teams with separate `team.yaml` files, which is multi-tenancy by construction.

**Position: The RFC must either commit to per-tenant rosters now (separate registration namespace, separate evidence store, separate audit) or amend §Goals to declare single-tenant only and force a Stage-2 RFC for multi-tenant.** The middle path — "might support multi-tenancy later" — is where Teams shipped its worst tenant-isolation bug, per the Microsoft reviewer who lived it.

### The vocabulary and word-choice critique

The OSS-old-school reviewer carried this one largely alone but the critique is sharp enough to address directly. "Medallion" is rebranded data-engineering vocabulary. "Seam" is naming a write/read transition the system already does informally; if a reviewer can mentally rename "seam" to "Gateway-dispatched write hook" and nothing breaks, the novelty is naming. "Analyst skill" is a new glossary item reviewers must internalize before they can evaluate anything. The DocuSign reviewer drove the parallel word-choice critique on "evidence": the word implies non-repudiation and tamper-evidence under FRE 901/902 and ESIGN/UETA, but the RFC produces neither, and using "evidence" 24 times when the doc means "observation record" is a deposition trap.

**Position: The DocuSign critique is unambiguously correct and is a 30-minute global find-and-replace.** The old-school vocabulary critique is partially correct — "seam" can probably stay because the concept of "the boundary as a policy site" is genuinely novel relative to OpenClaw's existing surface — but "medallion / raw / processed / curated" should be renamed to "ingest / processed / exposed" and the stage-count-of-three should be explicitly justified or dropped.

### The process critique: stop posting

The OSS-old-school and OSS-process reviewers converged hard on this and the Amazon reviewer's "operability appendix" recommendation is in the same neighborhood. The RFC currently bundles three normative shifts. The PR reply reframes the architecture without folding the reframe into the doc. The harness plan ships ~6.5 hours of work — two review teams, mock datasets, perf harness, comparison reporter, Proof Shape table — on a cold RFC that no maintainer has reacted to. The plan acknowledges in writing that this is the "awaiting-maintainer-reaction stage" and proposes a second comment anyway.

**Position: Both reviewers are right. The five questions need to drop to two crisp normative questions, the harness scope needs to drop by ~80%, and the next PR comment should not be posted until a maintainer reacts.** This is the cheapest improvement in the whole memo.

## Recommendations

The following are ranked strictly by sequence — do these in order.

1. **Pause the test-harnesses work in its current scope.** This is the highest-leverage action because every other recommendation invalidates a portion of what the plan would have built. Stop before the 6.5 hours land. Effort: zero.

2. **Roll the governed-mode reframing into RFC 0010 as a v3 revision, in-doc, before any more PR comments.** Add an `## Implementation modes` section that distinguishes (a) in-process pipe substitution as policy-by-convention for hobbyist/single-claw deployments, (b) subprocess-with-FD-handoff as policy-by-construction for hosted/multi-tenant. Add a `## Drawbacks` section that leads with the bench multipliers (4× / 35× / 180×). Add a `## Prior art` section citing medallion, OPA/Rego, and service-mesh sidecars. These are pattern-match sections experienced reviewers expect. Effort: 2-3 hours.

3. **Split RFC 0010 into two RFCs and ship the boundary-line one first.** RFC 0010a: the control-plane / agent-plane boundary, with a real capability model named — every skill runs under a principal, every Policy artifact has a signer, process boundary at minimum, OS-enforced namespace preferred. RFC 0010b: pipeline-substrate shape with governed-mode opt-in. The boundary RFC is ackable in one round; the bundled RFC is not. Effort: 3-4 hours.

4. **Fix the four blocking architectural defects before any harness ships.** Specifically: (a) move TOCTOU from unresolved-question to normative text with the commit-evidence-then-commit-row sequence specified, (b) kill the timeout→pass escalation option and replace with timeout→block or timeout→bounded-queue, (c) flip the empty-evidence stamp default to fail-closed with cryptographic registry-hash binding, (d) invert the clarifying-prompts semantics so operator answers add analysts but cannot suppress them. Each fix is a paragraph in the RFC; together they close the four most-cited reviewer concerns. Effort: 2-3 hours.

5. **Ship the smallest runnable proof — one seam, one analyst, one bench number — instead of the full harness.** A 50-100 line Python proof that demonstrates Gateway dispatching an SSN-block analyst at one seam against synthetic chat-message ingress, with a single comparison bench number (pipeline-1-analyst vs pipeline-noop), posted as a runnable Gist link in the existing PR thread *only after* a maintainer reacts to the v3 RFC. The two review teams, Synthea data, Workday data, clarifying-prompts schema, shared-lib policies, comparison reporter, and Proof Shape table all wait for that signal. Effort: 1-2 hours when the moment comes, plus the discipline to wait for it.

Two non-actions worth naming: do not post a second PR comment until a maintainer reacts to the first; do not file RFCs 0012-0015 from the plan's placeholder table.

## Risks

The top three risks if the work continues as currently scoped:

**Risk 1: Silence, not rejection.** The likeliest failure mode is not a maintainer saying "no" — it is no maintainer saying anything. The current PR thread is a one-author monologue with a long reply and a pending second long comment. Maintainers triaging an RFC with three bundled normative shifts, eight deferred unresolved questions, a contradiction between the doc body and the most recent comment, and a six-hour harness landing on a cold thread will deprioritize it. The work has already passed the threshold where the social cost of engaging exceeds the value of engaging. The fix is in Recommendation 2 — fold the reframing into the doc, drop the questions to two, and trim the next comment to a paragraph.

**Risk 2: Shipping the harness validates the wrong design.** If the test-harnesses repo ships at its current scope with the clarifying-prompts seam, the multi-team policy composition surface, and the two-axis severity_map, those become de-facto contracts whether the parent RFC adopts them or not. The Microsoft, pentester, and OSS-process reviewers all surfaced versions of this. The harness as a Stage-1 evidence exercise is supposed to prove the *open RFC's* contract is buildable; the current harness proves three adjacent RFCs' worth of contract before maintainers ack the first one. The damage is reputational — reviewers who read the harness as advocacy disengage from the RFC.

**Risk 3: The four architectural defects ship into production-shaped code.** If the harness lands before TOCTOU is closed, before timeout→pass is killed, before empty-stamp is flipped, and before clarifying-prompts is inverted, the reference impl encodes four security defects that downstream implementations will copy. The pentester reviewer's framing is the right one: "every new mechanism should be evaluated against 'can the agent plane rewrite, race, or DoS this?' and if the answer is yes, the mechanism doesn't ship." Right now four mechanisms answer yes and the harness would ship all four.

## Appendix: FAQ

**Q1: You're recommending pausing the harness work after I already wrote a 6.5-hour plan and pre-drafted the follow-up PR comment. Isn't this just sunk-cost-fallacy advice running the other direction — abandoning real work because eight strangers had opinions?**

The strangers were not asked for opinions on whether to do the work; they were asked to review what was posted. Eight reviewers from non-overlapping traditions (regulated SaaS, hyperscaler infra, attestation engineering, two pentest schools, two OSS-process schools) converged on the same process critique without coordination: too much surface area on a cold thread. That convergence is itself evidence — not of whether the work is valuable (it is) but of when it should land (after a maintainer reacts). Pausing is not abandoning. The 6.5 hours of harness work becomes more valuable, not less, after the RFC v3 lands and a maintainer signals which direction they prefer. Spending the hours now risks building against a version of the architecture maintainers will ask you to revise.

**Q2: You took the side of "fix the four defects before more harness work." But two of those defects (capability model, TOCTOU mechanism) are major-effort items that touch the substrate's implementation, not just the doc. Aren't you setting a bar that delays the RFC by weeks?**

The bar is not "implement the capability model before the RFC merges." The bar is "name the mechanism in the RFC body." Naming costs paragraphs, not weeks. The current RFC text says the property holds and does not name how; the recommended fix is to commit on paper to a specific mechanism (process boundary, signed Policy artifacts, principal model) and let the implementation follow. The TOCTOU fix is the same shape — move the unresolved-question into a normative paragraph that says "the substrate guarantees evidence-before-read via single-writer commit-evidence-then-commit-row." Implementation is a follow-up. The Amazon reviewer's framing applies: "I'd rather see a slower, correct number than a fast, racy one" — and right now the bench measures the fast racy number.

**Q3: The OSS-old-school reviewer told you to drop "analyst skill" and "seam" vocabulary and use boring names. You partially disagreed. Why are you keeping "seam" when a reviewer who has seen this pattern more times than you said it's dead weight?**

The reviewer's test is the right one — "if a reviewer can mentally rename your novel thing to a thing we already have and nothing breaks, the novelty is naming." Applying that test honestly to "seam": the concept of *the boundary as a policy site* is not "write hook." A write hook fires on a write; a seam is defined by the read-after-write transition where a third party that is neither producer nor consumer can interpose. The empty-evidence stamp, the multi-analyst composition, the visibility-after-evidence rule are all properties of seams, not write hooks. "Medallion" fails the test (raw/processed/curated is rebranded data-engineering vocabulary that adds no novel constraint to OpenClaw). "Seam" passes. The right move is the partial concession: rename "medallion" and the stage names, keep "seam," and stop saying "evidence" where the doc means "observation."

**Q4: You said "the customer is a single maintainer with a triage queue." What if no maintainer ever engages? At what point does that customer-framing become a counsel of paralysis?**

Two weeks of maintainer silence after the original 2026-06-09 reply is normal RFC behavior, not a deadlock. The OSS-process reviewer's etiquette pattern is the right one: ping once at the two-week mark with a short bump comment, then if silence continues past four weeks, write a private email to the maintainer most likely to care, asking specifically whether the project wants this direction at all. If the answer to that email is silence or "not now," the RFC is done and the right move is to fork the boundary-line argument into a separate internal proposal for sakAI's own use of OpenClaw — that work has value independent of upstream merge. The counsel-of-paralysis case requires roughly six weeks of no maintainer signal across two private and one public outreach. Before that, the customer framing holds.

**Q5: The DocuSign reviewer wants you to rename "evidence" to "observation record" globally. But the bench REPORT, the PR reply, the harness plan, and the test-harnesses repo all use "evidence." Isn't a global rename now going to look like an admission of weakness rather than a correction?**

It looks like a correction because that is what it is, and corrections post-bench are exactly the kind of credibility-building moves the Amazon and OSS-process reviewers separately endorsed about the bench numbers themselves. The author posted bench numbers that undercut the RFC's headline and then publicly reframed the proposal — that move increased reviewer trust, not decreased it. A rename from "evidence" to "observation" in v3 with an explicit `§Vocabulary` note ("evidence is reserved for the cryptographic-chain follow-up RFC; this document uses observation") is the same shape: honest about the boundary between what is delivered and what is claimed. The optical risk is real but it is much smaller than the deposition risk of leaving "evidence" in a document that does not deliver evidence properties.

**Q6: You ranked "pause the harness" as Recommendation 1. But the harness is currently the strongest signal you can send to maintainers that this proposal has a reference implementation and is serious. Pausing it might read as cooling commitment. How do you justify the optics?**

The optics are inverted. A 6.5-hour harness on a cold RFC reads as "this author will make this expensive to merge" — the Amazon and OSS-old-school reviewers both surfaced versions of this directly. The harness is a strong signal *after* a maintainer engages, and a noise signal *before*. The Rust/TC39/KEP precedent the plan itself cites is to ship reference impls after a maintainer has signaled the proposal is going somewhere. The right commitment signal at this stage is the v3 RFC revision with the governed-mode reframing folded in — that costs 2-3 hours, is unambiguously responsive to the bench evidence, and gives maintainers something cheap to react to. The harness becomes the second strong signal, in sequence, not the first.

**Q7: Three reviewers (Microsoft, pentester, OSS-process) flagged the clarifying-prompts seam as "smuggled in." But the harness plan claims this is the surface where OpenClaw beats Presidio/Purview/Macie/Cloud DLP — the actual product differentiator. Are you really recommending dropping a differentiator on process grounds?**

The differentiator is *operator-intent-capture at config time*, not end-user interactive prompting at upload time. The Microsoft reviewer's Teams scar tissue is precise: end-users cannot answer "does this contain PHI?" and the regulatory exposure when they click "no" is the operator's. The pentester reviewer made the security case: any path where attacker-influenced metadata suppresses an analyst is a confused-deputy by design. Both of those critiques target the upload-time interactive version. The operator-attestation version — operator declares at config time "this Gateway processes HIPAA-covered data, jurisdiction = US," policy engine reads the constant — keeps the differentiator and kills the bypass. The recommendation is to invert the semantics, not drop the surface. And the OSS-process critique still holds: even the inverted version belongs in its own RFC, not smuggled through the harness.

**Q8: The pentester reviewer flagged that "governed mode is just a sidecar analog" without kernel-enforced isolation is policy-by-convention, not a real boundary. You agreed. But every reviewer also endorsed governed-mode as the strongest idea in the package. Aren't you trying to have it both ways?**

Yes, and the two-mode framing in Recommendation 2 is exactly how. Governed-mode is the right *architectural pattern* — opt-in per claw, Gateway-mediated, claw unaware — regardless of how the mediation is enforced. For the hobbyist-parent use case the pentester explicitly carved out ("for parental-monitoring on a hobbyist's machine that's probably fine"), in-process pipe substitution is sufficient because the threat model is "agent goes off the rails," not "agent is actively adversarial." For the hosted multi-tenant use case the same reviewer flagged as misframed, subprocess-with-FD-handoff under a different uid with seccomp constraints is required because the threat model is adversarial-by-default. Both modes share the same Policy declaration surface and the same evidence shape; what differs is the enforcement primitive. That distinction is the honest version of the reframing, and it is what should land in the v3 RFC's `Implementation modes` section. The mistake the current PR reply makes is asserting one mechanism (pipe substitution) and one analog (Istio) and pretending they fit both use cases — they don't, and the panel caught it.
